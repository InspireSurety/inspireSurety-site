Maintenance Bond Guide

When a construction project is declared complete, true protection continues after turnover with a maintenance bond. Also called a warranty bond or maintenance warranty bond, this surety instrument ensures contractors remain responsible for fixing defects that appear during the post‑completion warranty period.

Need more construction bond resources? Visit our Contractors & Construction Bond Hub for guides on bid, performance, payment, and subdivision bonds.

What a Maintenance Bond Is
A maintenance bond (sometimes labeled a warranty bond) guarantees a contractor will repair or replace defective work or materials for a specified period after project closeout. A construction maintenance bond typically protects the project owner — whether a city, county, private developer, or agency — against workmanship failures, faulty materials, and design defects discovered after completion.

Maintenance bond duration: commonly 1–2 years, though contract terms may require longer periods.

Who it protects: project owners and the public, including cases where a municipal maintenance bond is required for public works.

What it covers: corrective repairs for contractor‑caused defects; it does not cover normal wear and tear, misuse, accidents, or force majeure events.

Typical Uses and Examples
Road projects: repair of cracks, potholes, or failing drainage covered by a maintenance bond.

Building projects: fixing roof leaks or HVAC failures within the warranty window under a construction maintenance bond.

Public infrastructure: replacement or repair of sidewalks, streetlights, or traffic signals under a municipal maintenance bond.

Landscaping and site work: remediation of dead plantings or erosion problems covered by a maintenance warranty bond.

Claims, Limits, and Exclusions
Filing a maintenance bond claim requires documentation of the defect, contract terms, and proof contractor notification.

Sureties investigate claims and pay for covered repairs up to the bond amount if the contractor fails to remedy the defect.

Exclusions typically include normal wear and tear, vandalism, improper use, and events outside the contractor’s control.

Cost, Requirements, and Contract Planning
Maintenance bond cost varies with project size, contractor credit, experience, and the bond amount; contractors should budget bond premiums into bids.

Maintenance bond requirements are specified in contract documents and public procurement rules; some agencies mandate specific bond language or minimum durations.

Owners and contractors should clarify the maintenance bond duration, claim procedures, and responsibilities before contract award.

Maintenance Bond vs Performance Bond
A maintenance bond covers post‑completion defects for a warranty period.

A performance bond guarantees the contractor will complete the work to contract standards during construction.

Projects often require both bonds: the performance bond for delivery and a maintenance bond to protect long‑term quality.

Practical Advice: Procurement and Risk Management
Confirm agency or contract maintenance bond requirements early and include bond costs in estimates.

Work with a surety experienced in construction placements to obtain competitive premiums and appropriate coverage.

Keep clear punch lists, as‑built records, and maintenance logs to speed maintenance bond claim resolution if problems arise.

Final Takeaway
A properly structured maintenance bond or warranty bond is a vital element of construction risk management. Understanding maintenance bond cost, coverage limits, maintenance bond duration, and how it differs from a performance bond helps owners, contractors, and public agencies protect investments and deliver durable projects.
