What Is an Appeal Bond (Supersedeas Bond)?



An appeal bond, also known as a supersedeas bond or court appeal bond, is a court‑required surety bond that guarantees the appellant will pay the full judgment, court costs, and damages if the appeal is unsuccessful. Courts require this bond to prevent delays in payment and ensure the appellee is protected during the appeal process.



This bond protects the court and opposing party from:



Failure to pay the judgment after losing the appeal

Delayed payment damages

Court‑ordered fees and costs

Bad‑faith appeals or attempts to avoid payment

If you are filing an appeal and need to stay enforcement of a judgment, the court will likely require an appeal/supersedeas bond before granting the stay.



Appeal Bond Amounts and Cost

Appeal and supersedeas bond amounts are typically set by the court and often equal:



100%–150% of the judgment amount

Additional court costs

Interest during the appeal

Who Needs an Appeal or Supersedeas Bond?

You may need an appeal bond or supersedeas bond if you are:



Appealing a civil court judgment

Requesting a stay of execution during appeal

A business contesting a financial ruling

An individual appealing a monetary judgment

A defendant seeking to delay payment until the appeal is resolved

Any party appealing a judgment that involves financial damages may be required to file this bond.



Appeal Bond Requirements \& Eligibility

Courts typically require:



A filed notice of appeal

Court‑mandated bond amount

Credit and financial review

Collateral for large or high‑risk judgments

Annual renewal if the appeal extends beyond one year

Some courts require the bond to be filed within a strict timeframe after the judgment—often 10 to 30 days.
